Agile

Agile methodology is based upon 12 principles:

  1. Continuously deliver the software as features are implemented to satisfy the customer and make changes on demand.
  2. Encourage changes to the requirements even in late development.
  3. Working software is delivered frequently (weeks rather than months)
  4. Close, daily cooperation between business people and developers
  5. Projects are built around motivated individuals, who should be trusted
  6. Face-to-face conversation is the best form of communication (co-location)
  7. Working software is the principal measure of progress
  8. Sustainable development ie sponsors, developers, and users should be able to maintain a constant pace indefinitely.
  9. Continuous attention to technical excellence and good design
  10. Simplicity—the art of maximizing the amount of work not done—is essential
  11. The best architectures, requirements, and designs emerge from self-organizing teams.
  12. Teams regularly reflects on how to become more effective, then tunes and adjusts its behavior accordingly.

In response to the problems that the waterfall model presented, a group of software engineers discussed lightweight development methodologies in 2001 and decided to use the term Agile and wrote the twelve principles of agile software development as seen above.

Inspired by the agile principles, there was other software development methodologies such as "Extreme Programming", which follows various rules including the following:

  1. Small releases
  2. Project is divided into iterations
  3. Stand up meeting starts each day
  4. Refactor whenever and wherever possible
  5. Customer is always available
  6. All production code is pair programmed
  7. Integrate often
  8. All code must have unit tests
